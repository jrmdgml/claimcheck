The Rating System

Three ratings. Plain language. No guesswork.

Every claim in our database gets one of three ratings based on what the scientific evidence actually says. Here is what each one means for your shopping decision.

GREEN

The evidence is solid.

Multiple high-quality, independent studies consistently support this claim at the ingredient, dose, and mechanism stated on the label. This is what good looks like.

What it means for you

The health claim is supported by independent science. You can buy with confidence that the label reflects what the research says.

ORANGE

The evidence is mixed or limited.

Some research supports the claim, but not enough to be definitive. Studies may be small, preliminary, or inconsistent. The science is promising but not settled.

What it means for you

The claim is plausible but not proven. You are not being misled—but the scientific case is not as strong as the label might suggest. Proceed with that in mind.

RED

The evidence does not support the claim.

The peer-reviewed literature does not back up what the label says. The ingredient may not work, the dose may be ineffective, or the claimed mechanism has no credible scientific basis.

What it means for you

The claim is not scientifically supported. That does not mean the product is harmful, but the health claim on the label is not backed by the evidence.

The Context

Why nobody checked until now

The rules allow it

A 1994 law called DSHEA—the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act—allows supplement manufacturers to make health claims without getting approval from the FDA first. They just have to notify the FDA after the fact, and enforcement is extremely rare. The result is a market where manufacturers are essentially allowed to say whatever they want, as long as the wording follows a specific format.

The disclaimer is not a warning

You have seen it on every supplement bottle: “This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.” Most people assume this is a legal formality. It is actually a direct admission that nobody has verified the claim is scientifically valid. It is required by law precisely because the claim has not been checked.

Legal claims can still be false

The FDA reviews whether a claim is formatted correctly—not whether it is true. A supplement can legally claim to “support bowel regularity” even if there is no science behind that claim, as long as the wording is phrased in the right way. That is the gap ClaimCheck exists to fill.

You deserved better information

Americans spend over $50 billion a year on supplements, making decisions based largely on what the label says. Until now, there was no independent, science-based resource to check whether those labels were telling the truth. That is what ClaimCheck is built to be.
